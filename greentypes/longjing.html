Longjing Tea

✢ ✢

Longjing tea (龙井茶), also known as Dragon Well tea, is a famous green tea from Hangzhou, Zhejiang Province, China, and is one of China's most celebrated teas. Unlike Japanese steamed green teas like sencha, Longjing is pan-fired during processing, giving it its characteristic flat, sword-shaped leaves and distinctive chestnut-like flavor with sweet undertones.

Tang and Song Dynasty Origins
Longjing tea's history dates back over 1,200 years to the Tang Dynasty (618-907), though it gained significant prominence during the Song Dynasty (960-1279). The tea takes its name from the Dragon Well (Longjing) near Hangzhou, which according to legend, is connected to the sea and home to a dragon that controls rainfall. During the Song Dynasty, Longjing tea became part of imperial tea culture.
Qing Dynasty Imperial Recognition
Longjing tea achieved its highest status during the Qing Dynasty, particularly under Emperor Qianlong (1735-1796). The emperor was so enamored with Longjing tea during his visits to Hangzhou that he conferred imperial status upon 18 tea bushes before the Dragon Well. This royal endorsement established Longjing as a tribute tea and cemented its reputation as China's premier green tea.
Ming and Qing Processing Innovation
The distinctive flat shape and pan-firing technique that characterize modern Longjing tea were developed during the Ming and Qing dynasties. Tea masters around West Lake perfected the hand-pressing and pan-firing methods that create Longjing's unique appearance and flavor profile. These techniques have been passed down through generations and remain largely unchanged today.
Modern Protected Status
In the contemporary era, Longjing tea has received numerous protected designations including China Famous Tea status and geographical indication protection. The authentic production area is strictly defined, and traditional processing methods are preserved as intangible cultural heritage. Despite modernization, the highest grades continue to be handmade using centuries-old techniques.

Cultivation and Terroir
Authentic Longjing tea grows in the specific microclimate around West Lake in Hangzhou, where the combination of misty mountains, abundant rainfall, and mineral-rich soil creates ideal growing conditions. The tea bushes are typically Camellia sinensis var. sinensis, and the best leaves come from early spring harvests. The unique terroir of the Zhejiang region is considered essential for true Longjing character.
Precision Harvesting
Longjing tea harvesting follows strict seasonal timing, with the pre-Qing Ming (before April 5) harvest considered the highest quality (Ming Qian Longjing). Only the tender bud with one or two young leaves are picked, typically by hand. The timing is crucial - leaves picked too early lack flavor development, while those picked too late become coarse and bitter.
Traditional Pan-Firing
The signature processing step involves pan-firing the leaves in a wok at temperatures around 80-100°C. This skilled process, called "sha qing" (killing the green), stops oxidation while shaping the leaves. Master tea makers use ten distinct hand movements to press, toss, and smooth the leaves, creating the characteristic flat, sword-like shape. This manual process requires years of experience to perfect.
Grading and Sorting
After the initial firing, the tea undergoes careful grading and sorting. Premium grades may undergo additional firing at lower temperatures to enhance flavor and ensure proper drying. The final moisture content must be precisely controlled to around 3-5% for optimal preservation. Each batch is evaluated for color, aroma, leaf integrity, and flavor potential before packaging.

‿̩͙⊱༒︎━━━━━━﹙Types and Classification﹚━━━━━━༒︎⊰‿̩͙

Quality Grades by Harvest Time
Ming Qian (Pre-Qing Ming): Harvested before Qingming Festival (early April), representing the highest quality with tender buds, light color, and delicate, complex flavor
Yu Qian (Pre-Rain): Harvested before Guyu (Grain Rain, around April 20), excellent quality with good balance of freshness and depth
San Xia (Third Summer): Later harvests with stronger flavor, more affordable pricing, and robust character suitable for daily drinking
Official Quality Grades
Superior Grade: Perfect flat shape, vibrant jade green color, uniform size, and exceptional aroma - typically reserved for gifts and special occasions
Grade 1: High quality with minimal imperfections, excellent flavor, and beautiful appearance
Grade 2: Good quality for everyday consumption, may have slight variations in leaf appearance
Commercial Grade: Basic Longjing character at affordable prices, suitable for restaurants and casual drinking
Regional Designations
Xihu (West Lake) Longjing: The original and most prestigious, from the designated protected area around West Lake
Qiantang Longjing: From the broader Hangzhou region, offering excellent quality and value
Yuezhou Longjing: From other parts of Zhejiang Province, representing the general style with regional variations
Special Categories
Shifeng Longjing: From Lion Peak Mountain, considered the pinnacle of Longjing quality
Meijiawu Longjing: From Meijiawu village, known for consistent high quality and traditional processing

‿̩͙⊱༒︎━━━━━━﹙Flavor Profile and Characteristics﹚━━━━━━༒︎⊰‿̩͙

Visual Appearance
Premium Longjing tea features flat, smooth, sword-shaped leaves with uniform pale jade green color. The leaves should be intact with minimal breakage and have a distinctive glossy appearance. When brewed, the leaves unfurl gracefully, revealing their完整性, while the liquor displays a light yellow-green hue with brilliant clarity.
Aroma Complexities
The dry leaf aroma presents delicate vegetal notes with distinct chestnut and toasted characteristics. High-quality Longjing exhibits subtle floral undertones and fresh, sweet hay notes. When brewed, the aroma becomes more complex, releasing layers of orchid, fresh beans, and sweet corn, with a clean, refreshing finish that lingers pleasantly.
Flavor Components
Toasted Nut Foundation: Distinctive chestnut and soybean notes derived from the pan-firing process, creating a warm, comforting base
Natural Sweetness: Inherent sweetness that balances the vegetal notes, particularly pronounced in Ming Qian harvests
Vegetal Freshness: Delicate flavors of fresh peas, steamed greens, and young corn that provide refreshing character
Floral Undertones: Subtle orchid and lilac notes that add complexity and elegance to the flavor profile
Clean Finish: Refreshing aftertaste that leaves the palate feeling clean and invigorated without astringency
Mouthfeel and Texture
High-quality Longjing has a smooth, rounded mouthfeel with medium body and no harshness. The liquor feels clean and refreshing in the mouth, with a satisfying weight that coats the palate without heaviness. The combination of umami from the amino acids and sweetness from the pan-firing creates a harmonious, lingering sensation that evolves through multiple infusions.

‿̩͙⊱༒︎━━━━━━﹙Health Benefits and Nutritional Profiles﹚━━━━━━༒︎⊰‿̩͙

Rich Antioxidant Content
Longjing tea contains high levels of catechins, particularly epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG), which provide powerful antioxidant protection. The pan-firing process preserves these beneficial compounds while developing unique flavor characteristics. Regular consumption helps combat oxidative stress, support cellular health, and may reduce the risk of chronic diseases.
Digestive Support
Longjing tea has traditionally been valued for its digestive benefits. The tea helps stimulate digestion, relieve feelings of fullness, and support healthy gut function. The combination of polyphenols and mild astringency makes it particularly beneficial when consumed after meals, and it has been used in traditional Chinese medicine for this purpose for centuries.
Mental Alertness and Relaxation
Like other green teas, Longjing contains a balanced combination of caffeine and L-theanine that promotes focused mental alertness without jitteriness. The L-theanine content induces alpha brain waves associated with relaxed concentration, making Longjing an ideal tea for meditation, study, or creative work that requires both calm and focus.
Metabolic and Weight Management
The catechins in Longjing tea, particularly EGCG, have been shown to support metabolic function and enhance fat oxidation. Regular consumption can contribute to healthy weight management when combined with balanced diet and exercise. The tea also helps regulate blood sugar levels, making it beneficial for metabolic health maintenance.
Oral Health and Fresh Breath
Longjing tea provides natural fluoride and contains antibacterial compounds that support oral health. The tea helps inhibit bacteria associated with plaque formation and bad breath, while the antioxidants support gum health. The refreshing quality of Longjing makes it an excellent natural breath freshener when consumed after meals.

‿̩͙⊱༒︎━━━━━━﹙Traditional Brewing Methods﹚━━━━━━༒︎⊰‿̩͙

Water Temperature and Quality
The ideal water temperature for Longjing ranges between 75-85°C (167-185°F), with premium Ming Qian grades benefiting from lower temperatures around 75°C. Water quality is crucial - soft, mineral-balanced spring water is preferred as hard water can mask the delicate flavors. Never use boiling water, as it will scorch the leaves and create bitterness.
Traditional Chinese Method
The classic Chinese method involves using a tall glass or gaiwan. Place 3-5 grams of leaves in the vessel, add hot water, and observe the leaves dance and unfurl ("the ageless tea play"). This method allows full appreciation of the leaves' appearance and aroma evolution. The glass walls conduct heat evenly, allowing precise temperature control.
Multiple Infusion Technique
High-quality Longjing can yield 3-4 excellent infusions, each revealing different characteristics:

First infusion (30-45 seconds): Highlights delicate aromas and initial sweetness
Second infusion (20-30 seconds): Reveals the core flavor profile and complexity
Third infusion (30-45 seconds): Shows lingering sweetness and subtle undertones
Each subsequent infusion requires slightly longer steeping times to extract remaining flavors.
Teaware Selection
While Longjing can be brewed in various vessels, traditional preferences include:

Glassware: Allows visual appreciation of leaves and liquor color
Gaiwan: Porcelain lidded bowl offering excellent heat control and aroma concentration
Yixing Clay: For experienced drinkers, as the porous clay absorbs flavors over time
The vessel choice significantly impacts the tea's expression and should be matched to the drinking context.
Grandpa Style Brewing
A popular casual method involves placing leaves in a tall glass, adding hot water, and drinking directly while leaves settle at the bottom. This "grandpa style" allows continuous extraction and evolving flavor throughout the drinking experience. It's particularly suited for appreciating how Longjing's character changes as water temperature decreases.

‿̩͙⊱༒︎━━━━━━﹙Cultural Significance and Traditions﹚━━━━━━༒︎⊰‿̩͙

Imperial Legacy and Tribute Tea Status
Longjing tea's association with Emperor Qianlong established its position as China's premier green tea. The emperor's legendary visits to Hangzhou, where he personally picked tea leaves and composed poems about Longjing, created an enduring cultural legacy. This imperial endorsement made Longjing a symbol of refinement and cultural sophistication that persists today.
Literary and Artistic Connections
Longjing tea has inspired centuries of Chinese poetry, calligraphy, and painting. Scholars and artists gathered in West Lake tea houses to drink Longjing while creating works that celebrated its beauty and elegance. The tea became associated with intellectual pursuit and artistic creation, embodying the Confucian ideal of cultivated refinement.
Modern Diplomatic Role
In contemporary China, Longjing tea serves as an important diplomatic gift and symbol of Chinese culture. World leaders and foreign dignitaries are often presented with premium Longjing during state visits. The tea represents Chinese hospitality, cultural heritage, and the art of living, making it an ambassador of Chinese tea culture worldwide.
Seasonal Celebration and Tourism
The annual Longjing harvest season attracts tourists and tea enthusiasts to Hangzhou for fresh tea experiences. Tea-picking activities, processing demonstrations, and tasting events celebrate the cultural significance of Longjing tea. This seasonal celebration connects modern urban dwellers with agricultural traditions and reinforces Longjing's status as a living cultural treasure.
